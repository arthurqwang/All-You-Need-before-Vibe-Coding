Chapter 1: The thing about computer hardware and software

Opening remarks

Before we start talking about AI programming, we need to figure out one of the most basic questions: what is this computer thing?

The word computer has a wider meaning than a computer, which generally refers to a desktop computer for personal use. In addition to computers, computers include other types of computers, such as mainframe computers, servers, mobile phones, and even quantum computers, among many others.

You use computers every day, cell phones, but do you know how it works? Do you know why it counts, plays video, plays games?

In this chapter, we will nag the basic principles of computers in vernacular. Instead of all those complicated circuit diagrams and formulas, talk about what you can understand.

You understand the basics of the computer so you can understand what the program is about and what the AI code is giving you.

1.1 What can and cannot be done by a computer?

1. Computers are not gods.

Let's start with the big truth: a computer is not a fairy, it's a tool.

A lot of people think computers are smart and can do a lot of things. In fact, the computer is not smart at all, it can only do one thing: arithmetic.

Add, subtract, multiply, divide, 0 and 1. that's all.

But it counts so fast that you don't feel it.

For example:

Look at the video. What's the computer doing? It's counting. Decode the data in the video files into images and sound.

You play games, what is the computer doing? Counting. How the calculation screen is displayed, how the characters move, and how the collision is handled.

You're online, what's the computer doing? Still counting. How the data is transmitted, how the web pages are rendered.

You write the code with AI, what's the computer doing? Or is it counting. The text you enter, the parameters of the model, the code of the output.

So computers are essentially "fast iron boxes."

2. What can computers do?

What a computer can do, it can be converted into a calculation, it can be counted as doing:

Storage Information

Save the data as 0s and 1s. Your archived documents, photos, and videos are all 0s and 1s in the eyes of your computer.

Processing of data

Various calculations are made of the data. Add-and-decreases, comparison sizes, and logical judgements are all calculations.

Sending data through the network to others. How you pack, how you transmit, how you verify, is a calculation.

These things, they're all counts.

Things that computers can't do are things that can't be converted into calculations.

Before there was no large language model, computers could not do the following things, but now it is a bit difficult to say, if general artificial intelligence is realized, the following can be done. For now, I still think it can't be done.

Can't create

Computers don't "invent" things, they can only process data according to the rules you give them.

You give it a bunch of data that can help you analyse, summarize, predict, but it doesn't create new things in the air.

Has anyone said that AI can write? Can he write articles, can he draw pictures, can he code?

Unable to judge

Computers don't feel good or bad or wrong, they just do it on terms.

You give it a judgment that returns to True or False, but it doesn't know what that judgement means, but it's just carrying out the logical calculations.

1.2 CPU, memory, hard drive, GPU - What do they do?

The four big pieces of a computer:

Open a computer with a lot of parts. But the core is basically four: CPU, memory, hard drive, GPU (if you can not.

The four brothers had their own division of labor, and with the cooperation of work, the computer could run.

Let's one-on-one.

1. CPU: Brain

CPU, it's called the Central Processor, and you can interpret it as a computer brain.

What's CPU doing?

It is responsible for executing instructions, performing calculations, and controlling the work of other parts.

You open a software, CPU reads the instructions, and you do one. You click a button, CPU handles your click, and you tell the screen what to show.

CPU characteristics:

You can do anything, but you can't do much at once.

Like a person, you can take any job, but you can only do one thing at a time. You want it to do ten things at once? It has to come piece by piece, or switch to switch.

How's CPU? Look at some of the indicators:

Number of cores: a few brains. Two cores are two brains, four cores are four brains, and eight cores are eight brains. With more brains, you can do more work at the same time.

Main frequency: How fast does the brain turn? 30 GHz is three billion times a second. Fast is fast, fast is fast.

Cache: A small book next to the brain. Remembering the most recent data, you don't have to go to the warehouse every time. Cache is good, efficient.

For example:

Suppose you want to count 1000 mathematical problems.

The single nuclear CPU is like a person, counting together. The eight nuclear CPU is like eight people, counting 125 each, counting at the same time.

Which one is fast? Of course eight are fast at the same time.

But not all missions can be broken down into eight. Some have a sequence, and they have to come together, and at that point the eight is not much faster than the single.

2. Memory: workstation

Memory, all called "random access memory," you can interpret it as a computer workstation.

What's the memory for?

It temporarily keeps the data and procedures in use.

You open a piece of software, and the software's code and data are loaded into memory. The CPU reads the data from memory, processes it, and writes the result back to memory.

Features of memory:

Fast, but not durable.

How close are we?

The CPU reads the data from memory, blinks; it reads the data from the hard disk, half a day (on the time scale of the computer).

What do you mean, it's not going to last?

When the power is off, it's gone. Did the document you were writing lose power without saving it? All the data in the memory was gone and could not be retrieved.

Memory is good, depending on capacity:

Capacity: how big the workbench is, measured in bytes.

8 GB is 8 guib, 16 guib, 32 guib.

G is the 9th power of 10, that is, 1 billion; B is a byte, which can be simply understood as a letter and a Chinese character.

The workstations are big, there are more programs that can be put on simultaneously, and many software can't be built.

Have you ever been in a situation where:

We've got a lot of web pages and software, computers slow down, even hinting at "insufficiencies"?

That is, the workbench is full, and the CPU has no place to put new things. At this time, the operating system will move some temporarily unused data to the hard disk (this is called "virtual memory"), freeing up workbench space. But the hard drive is much slower than the memory, so the computer becomes a card.

3. Hard drive: warehouse

Hard drive, you can interpret it as a computer warehouse.

What does a hard drive do?

It permanently stores data and programs.

The movies you download, the photos you save, and the software you install are all on your hard drive. If you turn it off, you won't lose it.

Features of the hard drive:

Last but slow.

What does durability mean?

After the power was cut off, the data was still there, and it would be fine for a few years. The documents you archived in 2010 can still be opened now, which is the credit of the hard drive.

How slow are you?

That's why computers, when working, move the usual data from hard disks to memory, so that CPUs can read it from memory instead of from hard disks every time. That's how it works.

The hard drive is divided into two categories:

Mechanical Hard Disk (HDD)

There's a rolling disk, like an old recorder. When you read and write data, the head moves on the disk.

Advantages: Cheap, large capacity. Hundreds of dollars can buy several TBs.

Shortcomings: Slow, noise, fear of vibration.

Like an old warehouse, things are too slow to pick up.

Solid-state Drive (SSD)

There are no spinning components, and data are stored with chips, like the UCD magnification version.

Advantages: fast, silent, earthquake-resistant.

Shortcomings: are expensive and relatively small (but are now growing).

Like a new warehouse, pick up fast but rent high.

Now the new computers are mostly SSDs, much faster. Turns on from a few minutes to a few seconds, and turns on the software.

4 GPU: He was a great painter and now he's an artificial intelligence Hercules.

GPU, full name "the graphic processor."

What's GPU doing?

It specializes in image and video processing.

You play games, watch videos, model 3Ds, and you're working with GPU. CPU gives the image data to GPU, and the GPU calculates the colour of each pixel point and then exports it to the screen.

Features of the GPU:

He's good at doing a lot of simple work at the same time.

A CPU is a brain that does one thing at a time (or switches between doing many things). GPUs are thousands of "small brains", each of which is weak but capable of working simultaneously.

For example:

You have to count 1 million simple additions.

CPU approach: a brain, counting together. A million roads, though fast, will be counted for a while.

GPU approach: 1000 small brains, 1000 per person, calculate together, and get it done in an instant.

That's the GPU advantage: parallel calculations.

So, GPU's not just playing games, drawing pictures, but running the AI model now depends on it. The AI model requires a lot of simple calculations (mixture multipliers), and the GPU is good at this.

Is it ok without a GPU?

Yes, but many things can't be done or are slow.

Play a game? No GPU, it's a PPT.

Run an AI? No GPU, not a day later.

What's so bad about GPU?

Visible: equals the GPU's own workstation. Large enough to handle larger images, larger models.

Number of cores: number of small brains. More cores, faster calculations.

1.3 Operating systems: large butlers of computers

What's the operating system for?

You turn on your computer and see the Windows, macOS, or Linux interface. This is the operating system.

The operating system is the big butler of computers, which manages all the resources of computers and allows the various software to function properly.

Without an operating system, your computer is a bunch of parts, nothing to do. With an operating system, you can install software, open files, surf online.

Let's say:

Computer hardware is like a building with rooms, utilities, elevators. But without the property, the building was a mess, and no one knew how to use it.

The operating system is the real estate, which manages all aspects of the building:

Allocation of rooms (RAM)

Management of utilities (hardware management)

Maintaining order (process management)

Provision of services (system call)

2. What's in the operating system?

The operating system is mainly concerned with these matters:

Manage Hardware

CPU, memory, hard disk, GPU, these hardware resources are uniformly allocated by the operating system. Which program uses how much memory and how much CPU time is determined by the operating system.

So, for example, the operating system is like a dispatcher and arranges for the use of hardware resources for each program.

Your files are on your hard drive, and the operating system is responsible for organizing the files, creating directories, and managing permissions. You can see folders, copy and paste files, all thanks to the operating system.

HTTP is an express transmission, and your text may be intercepted by an intermediary.

HTTPS encryption process:

• Browser requests the server's certificate

8.2 How do large language models work?

1. What does the "big" of a large language model mean?

Large language model, the key is "big" and "language".

Let's start with the “big”: the previous AI model was small, and tens of millions of dollars were big. Now, the LLM, it's a multi-billion or even trillion parameter. The GPT-4 is said to have 1.8 trillion.

What are the parameters? You can understand it as a model's memory cell or knob. Each parameter is a adjustable number, and the model is based on billions of such numbers to "remember" what it learned.

The more parameters, the more complex and sophisticated the pattern that the model can remember. But not all parameters are good — high training costs, slow running, easy to “mortality back”.

Again, "Language": These models are language-specific. They read text in volumes, learning language patterns, knowledge, logic, and even a certain degree of common sense.

2. Predict the core gameplay of the next word

The core gameplay of the big language model is so simple that you can't believe it:

Give it a text that allows it to predict the next most likely word.

As simple as that.

For example, you type in, "It's a real day," and the model predicts that the next word is probably "good" or "good" or "hot."

Then the model takes the predicted words and goes on to predict the next one: "It's a nice day" → Predicting → "ah" "It's a nice day" → Predicting → "," It's a nice day "→Predicting" me "→...

The entire dialogue process is "squeezed" out of such a word. It looks like you are thinking, but you are actually doing a high-speed "guessing game".

You might say, "Isn't that a high-level input method?

Yeah, that's right. But this input method looks at the entire Internet data (in trillion words), so it's very good at guessing, and it looks like it really understands.

When this "guessing game" is extreme, the guessing content is no longer a simple word, but a paragraph of logical, knowledgeable, and even seemingly thoughtful words.

3. Training and reasoning

Large language models have two phases: training and reasoning.

Training phase:

Use big data (languages on the Internet — web pages, books, papers, codes, dialogues ) for models, allowing models to practice “conjectures”.

Wrong guess, the model adjusts its parameters (the tens of billions of knobs) to make it more accurate next time.

This process requires great computing — tens of thousands of GPUs running for several months at a time with tens of millions of dollars in electricity.

Logic stage:

A trained model can be used to answer questions. You send a hint to the model, the model runs once, and the answer is out.

The reasoning does not need to be so great that a piece of GPU, or even CPU, can run (of course slower).

Every time you talk with ChatGPT or Claude, you're doing a line of reasoning -- the model produces a word-by-word answer based on your input.

4. What the hell is Token?

You may notice that the AI chat interface sometimes shows either "X tokens" or "maximum token limit" for hints.

Token is the basic unit of text processing for large language models. It is not " word " or " word " , but a more flexible segment.

For example: "It's a nice day" may be divided into ["today", "weather", "nice"] three tokens, or it may be divided into ["today", "weather", "true", "good"] four tokens.

The English version of “I love progressing” may be divided as follows: [I, “love”, “pro”, “gramming”).

The word segmentation methods of different models are different, but roughly follow the principle of "common words are completely retained, and unusual words are broken into small pieces".

generally speaking

• One Chinese hyphenate 1 to 2 token

• One English word 1-2 token

• 1000 tokens ≈ 750 English words, or about 500 Chinese characters

Understanding the Token concept is important because:

AI has contextual window limits (e.g. 8K, 32K, 128K token) that go beyond what is “unrememberable”.

The bill is also token-based, and the input output is expensive. So the length of the matter directly affects the wallet.

5. Illusion: Why does AI talk nonsense?

You can see with more AI: AI sometimes gives completely wrong information with particular confidence.

You ask it, “Who is the 2025 NBA champion?" It might give you a damn good answer - the names of the players, the scores, the MVPs - but it's all made up.

That's "hallucination."

Why? Because the model is essentially a "probation game." The rule it learns is, "When asked questions about XX, the usual answer format is XXX." So it produces content in this format.

But it doesn't “know” what the truth is. It has no knowledge base, no databases, only the statistical patterns that are learned from training data.

So:

• AI is not necessarily right — always checking.

• AI is more confident and careful - it doesn't have the concept of “uncertainty”

• The code is also tested — the code that AI wrote may seem right, but it runs wrong.

8.4 Chat with AI: The lessons of the hint

2. Basic techniques for writing instructions

Number one: Say what you want.

Bad way to ask: "Write a program for me."

Good question: "Help me write a calculator program with Python, support additions and subtractions, command line interactive, user input two numbers and operators, output results."

Second: Set the role.

Let AI play a specific role, and the answer will be more professional. "You are a senior Python developer, please help me review the code below..."

Third: Give me an example.

You want AI to produce a format, give it an example. It'll follow your format.

Fourth: Step by step.

Don't throw all the complicated tasks to AI. Let it be the first step. Check it out before it's the second step.

Fifth: AI is required to think step by step.

When you have a logical problem, insert the words “please think step by step” or “Let's think step by step”, and the quality of AI's response usually improves considerably.

3. Step-by-step techniques

Use System Tip Word: Some AIs (e.g. Claude, ChatGPT) support setting the System Hint, which is equivalent to giving AI a "background setting" to answer in this style from the beginning to the end.

The context of the dialogue is managed: AI remembers the content of the current dialogue, but it does not remember the other. So, when it comes to complex issues, it stays in one dialogue and does not open a new one.

File uploading: Many AIs now support uploading pictures, PDFs, code files, etc. Direct uploading files makes it easier to analyse than to paste.

Tools: AI can call plugins, search web pages, run codes, etc.

Don't treat AI like a god.

Although AI is strong, it's not almighty. You have to know the bottom line:

AI doesn't understand the code, it just sees a lot of it. "like" is not equal to "yes."

AI goes wrong, and sometimes it goes wrong. Always stay alert.

AI does not have a privacy concept. Do not send AI sensitive information such as passwords, keys, ID numbers, etc.

AI has a cut-off date for knowledge. It doesn't know what happens when it's trained.

It's important to think of AI as a “smart but less reliable assistant”, not as a “God Almighty”.
